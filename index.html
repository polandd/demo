Designed by Danny Poland and created with Cumbria Wildlife Trust.

Danny Poland and Cumbria Wildlife Trust have created a garden to support Nature’s Recovery. Native grasses and wildflowers, grown in Cumbria, prove that there is a magical alternative to the traditional lawn. Providing habitat and allowing biodiversity, nature has an opportunity to thrive. Buzzing and birdsong, flowers and butterflies impact so positively on our own wellbeing too. Children from Irthington Village School have written their wildlife wishes to hang on the branches of a stunning Cornus tree and have made a ‘Walking with Wildlife’ footpath which leads to a pond surrounded by slate, our local Lakeland stone. Wildlife friendly perennials and plants in pots fill the top end of the garden and surround the Bee & Bee. We hope our garden brings joy.

About Me

I have always had a passion for the great outdoors and nature, this is ever since I can remember. This interest has continued as I’ve grown. I realised a love of horticulture early on when I would plant things in my garden. Seeing seedlings germinate or cuttings take root fills me with awe. I am now an apprentice studying level 3 in horticulture. I thoroughly enjoy working with my local community, brightening up areas where I live to benefit both people and nature. I have recently become a trustee of Cumbria Wildlife Trust. I have a passion for wildlife and our natural world. We’re at a critical point and necessary changes are needed to increase biodiversity and turn the tide on the decline. Almost 90% of UK households have a garden, making for over 20 million gardens that cover a larger surface area than all 2,300 Wildlife Trust nature reserves combined. We are all able to make a difference. I would like to help people to help nature because there are benefits for both. Horticulture is changing and adapting to new challenges and subsequently new ideas.

Cumbria Wildlife Trust

Wild flowers and plants are not only beautiful – they're an essential part of our lives. They provide food for humans and animals, and are the basis for many medicines. Without plants bees, birds, butterflies and other wildlife wouldn't exist. Many of our native plants are disappearing. More than half of Cumbria and the UK's bee, butterfly and moth species have declined in the past 50 years. The reason for this rapid decline isn't totally understood, but over the last 75 years we’ve lost 97% of flower rich meadows. To halt and reverse these alarming declines, we urgently need to restore wild spaces and wildflowers for pollinators.Together, our gardens are a vast living landscape. With an estimated 24 million gardens in the UK, the way they are cared for can make a big difference to the natural world. - Cumbria Wildlife Trust.

Sourcing My Materials

In our exhibit, I bought everything I could locally, this was important to me and Cumbria Wildlife Trust to minimise environmental impacts. We also felt it important to support local businesses. I used repurposed, untreated wood for the frame of our exhibit, donated by The Rebuild Site CIC. There were no chemicals used in this project, it has been 100% organic. Our wildflowers were expertly grown at the Cumbria Wildlife Trust site at Gosling Sike in Houghton, while I grew the meadow grass. We only used peat-free compost; Sylva Grow is the only peat-free compost endorsed by the RHS and it worked really well. The mini meadow was created to show a beautiful and biodiverse alternative to the traditional lawn. I hope we prove that you don’t need a large space to encourage nature into your garden. We were able to loan most of the seed trays for growing the meadow grass and reused trays were used for the wildflowers. In the centre of the meadow is the ‘Walking with Wildlife’ footpath. This was created by children from Irthington Village School. At the end of the footpath, there are 4 main features. Firstly, the pond is made from recycled plastic. The stunning display of pots came from The Pot Place in Plumpton. Next to the pond, we have the Bee & Bee. The post is untreated wood from a sustainable source. The tiles are donated from The Rebuild Site and are reclaimed. On the other side of the pond is our ‘Wildlife Wishes’ tree. Irthington Village School children have written their wishes for wildlife and nature. They are tied onto the tree with biodegradable twine. I chose a wedding cake tree Cornus Controversa ‘Veriegata’. I adore this specimen, I had been admiring it for ages because it comes from Larch Cottage Nurseries, where I’m completing my level 3 apprenticeship in horticulture. Hopefully, it will be in flower for the show but nevertheless, the variegation and the copper-coloured stems are fantastic. The pot it sits in is weatherproof and goes extremely well with the colour of the stem and the branches, as before, it’s from The Pot Place in Plumpton. Last but not least is the log pile. This is important to insects for shelter and food. My neighbours have a magnificent and ancient horse chestnut tree, Aesculus hippocastanum. Its trunk began to split with age and we were worried. Subsequently, with some prompt action and tender care, an arborist came and rebalanced its weight. It did lose some branches but it is now standing strong. This log pile was some of the necessary wood it had to lose to be able to live on.
